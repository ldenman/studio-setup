Picked the simplest song I could think of to break in the studio. Bob Dylan's *Knockin' on Heaven's Door*. Three chords, straight time, around 68 BPM. Hard to mess up — which is exactly the point when you're testing a new recording setup.

## Two Guitar Tones

**Rhythm** got a Fender clean amp sim — bright, open, neck pickup with the tone rolled back. The kind of warm Strat sound where all the grit comes from the outboard chain downstream, not the amp. The Neve-style preamp adds body, the 1176 catches the pick attack, and the Distressor adds just enough compression to hold it together.

**Lead** went darker. A Mesa-style sim with the gain pulled way down — more of a thick, singing sustain than the usual high-gain snarl. The outboard chain adds its own character on top, so the amp sim doesn't need to do much.

Switching between the two tones is instant. The outboard chain stays the same either way; only the amp sim changes. One bus for rhythm, another for lead. Mute one, unmute the other.

## Vocal Chain

Full outboard path — Neve-style preamp for color, 1176 for transient control, optical leveling to smooth everything out. Added a doubler effect on the vocal mix bus with wide stereo spread. Gives it width without sounding obviously processed. The kind of thing where you'd miss it if it was gone but wouldn't notice it's there.

## The Backing Band

Logic's session players handled bass and drums. Added a sub-octave effect on the bass to push it into the midrange — gives it more presence alongside the guitar without muddying the low end.

## What We Learned

The biggest lesson had nothing to do with tone. It's the small operational things: making sure the DAW's input monitoring is off so you're hearing through the console with zero latency, checking that the Model 12's track modes are set correctly so playback actually flows to the right places, and verifying that both the live performance and the playback returns are reaching the mix bus without doubling.

A three-chord song was the right call. Simple enough to focus on the system instead of the performance. Now the studio is proven — time for something harder.
