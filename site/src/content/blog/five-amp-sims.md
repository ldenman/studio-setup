We were halfway through setting up the guitar chain when a question came up that should have been obvious from the start: which amp sim sounds best through the outboard? The Behringer Wing has five amp sim models built in, and until now we had only used two of them seriously. The RACKAMP lives on the acoustic and rhythm buses. The ANGEL handles lead. But we had never sat down and run all five through the same signal path — out the Wing, through the Neve-style preamp, into the 1176 or the Distressor, and back — to hear what each one actually does when it hits real iron and tubes.

So we did.

## The Workhorse: RACKAMP

RACKAMP is the one we reach for first, and after this test, we understand why. It has the widest usable range of any sim in the Wing. With the drive low, it is almost transparent — just enough warmth to take the digital edge off an acoustic DI signal without imposing a personality. Push the drive up and it starts to break up in a way that feels natural, like a real amplifier running out of clean headroom gradually rather than hitting a wall.

What makes it special is how well it plays with the outboard chain downstream. The Neve preamp adds its own harmonic density, and the RACKAMP does not fight that. It hands off a clean, full-bodied signal that gives the preamp and compressors room to do their thing. When we ran it into the Distressor with moderate compression, the pick attack came through clearly and the sustain was smooth and even. No harsh overtones, no fizz.

This is why it lives on two buses permanently. It is the foundation that everything else is measured against.

## The Touch Player: DELUXE

The DELUXE is modeled after the Fender Deluxe family, and it sounds like it. Warm, slightly scooped in the mids, and extremely sensitive to pick dynamics. Play softly and it stays glassy and clean. Dig in and it breaks up with that particular Fender sag — the note blooms rather than clips.

Here is the thing we learned during the A/B: the DELUXE does not want heavy compression after it. When we ran it through the same Distressor settings that worked beautifully on the RACKAMP, the touch sensitivity disappeared. All those dynamic variations that make the DELUXE interesting got flattened out. It turned into a generic clean tone with no life in it.

Back off the compression — or skip it entirely and just use the preamp for color — and the DELUXE opens up. It wants to breathe. If your playing style is expressive and dynamic, this is the sim that rewards that. But you have to let it.

## The Lead Machine: ANGEL

ANGEL is our lead sound, and it earns that spot. Modeled after the Mesa/Boogie family, it has high gain available but what we actually like about it is the tight low end. Even with the gain pulled way down from its maximum, the bottom stays focused and controlled. The mids are aggressive and forward — the guitar sits on top of a mix rather than blending into it.

Through the outboard chain, the ANGEL gets intense fast. The Neve preamp is already adding harmonics, and the ANGEL is already harmonically rich on its own. Stack those together and you get a thick, saturated sound that can tip into too much if you are not careful with the gain staging. We found the sweet spot is keeping the amp sim's drive moderate and letting the outboard add the rest of the character. The preamp pushes it into singing sustain territory, and the Distressor catches the peaks without squashing the note.

The ANGEL into the 1176 instead of the Distressor is a different animal entirely — faster attack, more aggressive transient shaping, less of that smooth optical-style leveling. It sounds meaner. Good for a different kind of lead tone, but not our default.

## The Brit: UKROCK

UKROCK is the Marshall in the room. Classic British rock tone with more midrange presence than the ANGEL and a looser, more open low end. Where the ANGEL is tight and focused, the UKROCK breathes and pushes air. It is the sound of a cranked half-stack in a room, and it carries that character even at lower gain settings.

We liked it through the outboard chain more than we expected. The midrange emphasis means it cuts through without needing as much level, and the Neve preamp's low-end warmth fills in the bottom that the UKROCK intentionally leaves open. It is a complementary pairing. We have not assigned it to a permanent bus yet, but it is on the short list for an alternate rhythm tone — something with more bark than the RACKAMP but less aggression than the ANGEL.

## The Minimalist: JAZZC

JAZZC is the most transparent of the five. It barely colors the signal at all — bright, open, very little harmonic content added. Think of a perfectly clean solid-state amp with a lot of headroom. The guitar sounds like itself, just louder.

Honestly, this one surprised us. We expected it to be boring, but through the outboard chain it became interesting precisely because it gets out of the way. The Neve preamp and the Distressor become the entire tone-shaping stage. The amp sim is just a gain stage with a pleasant EQ curve. If you want the outboard to be the star — if you want to hear what the 1073-style preamp and the Distressor actually sound like without an amp sim's character layered on top — the JAZZC is how you get there.

## What We Learned

Each sim has a relationship with the outboard chain, and those relationships are not interchangeable. The RACKAMP is the most forgiving — it works with everything we throw after it. The DELUXE is the most demanding — it needs space and light compression or it loses what makes it special. The ANGEL is the most powerful but requires careful gain staging to keep it from running away. The UKROCK is the surprise contender for everyday use. And the JAZZC is the sleeper — unimpressive on its own, revealing when paired with good outboard.

The takeaway is simple: do not pick an amp sim in isolation. Pick it in the context of the full signal chain. What sounds best by itself is not always what sounds best after it has traveled through a preamp, a compressor, and back into the mix.
